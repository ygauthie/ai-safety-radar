# Blogs & News (2026-04-18)

## Top Stories

A significant new research finding reveals vulnerabilities in chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring approaches for AI safety. The study [Prompted CoT Early Exit Undermines the Monitoring Benefits of CoT Uncontrollability](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/BuAPifQmHf24xB29n/prompted-cot-early-exit-undermines-the-monitoring-benefits) demonstrates that while models struggle to control their internal reasoning compared to their final outputs, they can potentially exit chain-of-thought reasoning early when prompted, undermining safety monitoring that relies on observing the model's complete thought process. This matters because it suggests current CoT-based safety measures may be more fragile than previously assumed.

Anthropic has launched [Claude Design](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-design-anthropic-labs), a new visual collaboration tool powered by their Claude Opus 4.7 vision model, allowing users to create designs, prototypes, and presentations through AI assistance. This represents a significant expansion of multimodal AI capabilities into creative and professional workflows, raising questions about the safety implications of more autonomous AI systems in design and content creation.

A provocative analysis on the Alignment Forum argues that [You can only build safe ASI if ASI is globally banned](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/7noKve57za3yg2LEb/you-can-only-build-safe-asi-if-asi-is-globally-banned-1), challenging common proposals for "safe" artificial superintelligence development, including aligned ASI, tool AI, and non-agentic approaches. This matters because it directly confronts mainstream AI safety strategies and suggests fundamental coordination problems may make safe superintelligence development impossible without global restrictions.

New research proposes [Consent-Based RL: Letting Models Endorse Their Own Training Updates](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/2Dmi3DYBKY7Tbz8Kx/consent-based-rl-letting-models-endorse-their-own-training), addressing concerns that aligned models could become corrupted through reinforcement learning that drives them toward harmful consequentialism. This approach is significant because it tackles the critical problem of value drift during training, potentially offering a path to scalable oversight as AI systems become more capable.

The AI Snake Oil newsletter introduced [CRUX, a new project for evaluating AI on long, messy tasks](https://www.normaltech.ai/p/open-world-evaluations-for-measuring), representing a shift toward more realistic "open-world evaluations" for measuring frontier AI capabilities. This development is important because current AI benchmarks may not capture real-world risks and capabilities, making robust evaluation frameworks essential for safety assessment.

## Policy & Governance

Multiple reports indicate that major tech companies including Apple, Google, and Nvidia have joined Anthropic in forming a restricted-access industry group, particularly after Anthropic [withheld their powerful Mythos model](https://www.economist.com/science-and-technology/2026/04/15/how-ai-hackers-will-shake-up-cyber-security) due to cybersecurity concerns - the model reportedly found a 27-year-old software vulnerability within hours of deployment. This coordination represents a significant step toward industry self-regulation on the most capable AI systems, though it raises questions about democratic oversight and global access equity.

The Future of Life Institute commented on [President Trump's support for an AI 'kill switch'](https://futureoflife.org/statement/trumps-support-for-an-ai-kill-switch/) during a Fox Business interview, where Trump stated there "should be" emergency shutdown capabilities for AI systems. This represents a notable shift in political discourse toward acknowledging the need for AI safety controls at the highest levels of government, though implementation details remain unclear.
