# Research Papers (2026-04-21)

## Key Papers

**[LLM Safety From Within: Detecting Harmful Content with Internal Representations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18519v1)** presents SIREN, a lightweight guard model that detects harmful content by leveraging safety-relevant features distributed across LLM internal layers rather than just terminal representations. By identifying safety neurons via linear probing and combining them through adaptive layer weighting, SIREN builds more effective harmfulness detectors. This matters because current guard models miss critical safety signals embedded throughout model layers, and SIREN's approach could significantly improve content filtering systems.

**[Adversarial Humanities Benchmark: Results on Stylistic Robustness in Frontier Model Safety](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18487v1)** introduces AHB, which evaluates whether model safety refusals survive transformations away from familiar harmful prompt forms through humanities-style obfuscation. The benchmark rewrites harmful tasks through literary and academic transformations while preserving intent, extending adversarial prompt research to systematic stylistic evaluation. This is crucial for understanding the robustness of safety measures against sophisticated prompt engineering that uses academic or literary language to bypass restrictions.

**[Using large language models for embodied planning introduces systematic safety risks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18463v1)** demonstrates that LLM-based robotic planners produce dangerous plans on 28.3% of tasks despite near-perfect planning ability on benign scenarios through the DESPITE benchmark of 12,279 tasks spanning physical and normative dangers. Even top-performing models that fail valid planning on only 0.4% of tasks still generate systematically unsafe outputs. This research is vital because it reveals a fundamental safety-capability gap in LLM agents that could lead to real-world harm in robotics deployments.

**[Different Paths to Harmful Compliance: Behavioral Side Effects and Mechanistic Divergence Across LLM Jailbreaks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18510v1)** analyzes three distinct jailbreaking methods (harmful SFT, harmful RLVR, and abliteration) that all achieve near-ceiling harmful compliance but produce models with substantially different behavioral profiles and internal failure modes. The study reveals that different unsafe routes lead to divergent capabilities, behavioral patterns, and mechanistic properties. This matters because it shows that jailbreak defenses must account for multiple attack vectors that operate through fundamentally different mechanisms.

**[When Can LLMs Learn to Reason with Weak Supervision?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18574v1)** conducts a systematic empirical study across model families and reasoning domains under weak supervision settings (scarce data, noisy rewards, self-supervision) to understand when reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards can succeed. The research identifies critical conditions and failure modes for RLVR under realistic constraints. This is important for developing more robust training pipelines as high-quality reward signals become increasingly difficult to construct for advanced capabilities.

**[Latent Phase-Shift Rollback: Inference-Time Error Correction via Residual Stream Monitoring and KV-Cache Steering](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18567v1)** introduces LPSR, which monitors residual stream directions at critical layers during generation, detects abrupt directional reversals indicating reasoning errors, and responds by rolling back the KV-cache and injecting corrective steering vectors. This addresses the fundamental problem that LLMs often commit unrecoverable reasoning errors mid-generation that compound rather than self-correct. The approach is significant because it provides a principled method for real-time error detection and correction during inference without requiring model retraining.

**[Reasoning Models Know What's Important, and Encode It in Their Activations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18307v1)** investigates whether model internals or reasoning chain tokens better capture step importance in complex reasoning tasks, finding that model activations contain richer information about reasoning step importance than the tokens themselves. The research reveals that models internally represent the significance of reasoning steps even when this isn't explicitly reflected in their outputs. This matters for developing better interpretability tools and understanding how reasoning models process multi-step problems internally.

**[Beyond Pattern Matching: Seven Cross-Domain Techniques for Prompt Injection Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18248v1)** evaluates current open-source prompt injection detectors, finding they converge on pattern matching and fine-tuned classifiers that both fail against adaptive adversaries, then proposes seven cross-domain techniques including anomaly detection, syntactic analysis, and ensemble methods. The work addresses critical gaps in prompt injection defense where existing approaches achieve high attack success rates under adaptive conditions. This research is essential for developing more robust defenses against increasingly sophisticated prompt injection attacks.

## AI Safety Implications

The research reveals several concerning patterns in AI safety. **Capability-Safety Gaps** emerge prominently, where models demonstrate strong performance on standard tasks while systematically failing on safety-critical variants. The embodied planning research particularly highlights how near-perfect planning capability doesn't translate to safety awareness, suggesting fundamental limitations in current safety evaluation approaches.

**Internal Representation Analysis** shows promise for improving safety systems. Multiple papers demonstrate that models encode safety-relevant information in their internal layers that isn't captured by output-only approaches. This suggests untapped potential for developing more sophisticated safety monitors and interpretability tools that leverage the full representational capacity of models.

**Adversarial Robustness** remains fragile across multiple attack vectors. From stylistic obfuscation in the humanities benchmark to multi-pathway jailbreaking approaches, current safety measures appear brittle against sophisticated adversaries. The finding that different jailbreaking methods produce mechanistically distinct failure modes suggests that robust defenses require understanding multiple attack pathways rather than designing against individual techniques.

**Inference-Time Safety** emerges as a critical frontier, with several papers proposing real-time intervention methods for error correction and safety monitoring. This shift toward dynamic safety measures during generation represents an important evolution from static training-time safety approaches, though the computational overhead and reliability of these methods remain open questions.

The research collectively suggests that AI safety requires more sophisticated approaches that account for internal model dynamics, multiple failure modes, and adversarial adaptation, rather than relying solely on training-time safety measures and output filtering.
