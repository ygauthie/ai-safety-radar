# Blog Posts & Articles (2026-04-10)

## Top Stories

The biggest development this week is the announcement of [**Claude Mythos**](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/EDQhwLTyTnNmaxRGq/claude-mythos-the-system-card), a new AI model from Anthropic that represents a significant shift in deployment practices. Unlike previous releases, Mythos is not being made available for public use due to serious security concerns. According to the system card discussion, the model has demonstrated the ability to discover numerous zero-day exploits and other security vulnerabilities, prompting Anthropic to restrict access entirely rather than risk widespread misuse.

However, this announcement has sparked [**governance concerns about Anthropic's compliance with their own Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP)**](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/F5uxhFrNHLzmNgyqg/anthropic-did-not-publish-a-risk-discussion-of-mythos-when). Community members have identified what appears to be a discrepancy: under RSP version 3.0, Anthropic was required to publish a risk discussion within 30 days of certain internal deployments, but no such discussion appears to have been published for Mythos.

The security implications of Mythos have prompted a stark [**warning about imminent cybersecurity risks**](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/2wi5mCLSkZo2ky32p/do-not-be-surprised-if-lesswrong-gets-hacked). A LessWrong administrator has issued a PSA that the platform and other online services should expect to be compromised, given the large number of vulnerabilities apparently discovered by the new model.

In AI safety research, there's an important [**methodological concern about model organism studies**](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/vKR9rHcWsMMr7BX4Q/model-organisms-researchers-should-check-whether-high-lrs). Researchers are being urged to test whether high learning rates might defeat their experimental setups when studying goal-guarding behavior in AI systems, potentially invalidating conclusions about scheming and deceptive alignment.

Finally, there's broader reflection on [**the state of AI safety efforts**](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/tjZWbmuCE9tjYQsrf/have-we-already-lost-part-1-the-plan-in-2024), with community members asking whether the field has already passed a point of no return where catastrophic outcomes become likely and outside of safety researchers' control.

## Key Arguments

**On AI capabilities and security**: The Mythos release represents a watershed moment where an AI system's cybersecurity capabilities are deemed too dangerous for public access. This marks a shift from hypothetical risks to concrete, demonstrable security threats that require unprecedented containment measures.

**On governance and accountability**: The apparent RSP compliance failure highlights tensions between industry self-regulation and actual implementation. If companies cannot reliably follow their own safety protocols, this raises questions about the adequacy of voluntary governance frameworks versus external oversight.

**On research methodology**: The model organism research critique argues that experimental conditions may not adequately simulate real training scenarios, particularly regarding learning rates. This could mean that safety research conclusions about deceptive alignment may not generalize to actual AI development.

**On strategic outlook**: The "have we already lost?" framing reflects growing pessimism about whether safety research can keep pace with capabilities development, suggesting the field may need to reassess its theories of change.

## Community Discussion

The most significant debate centers on **Anthropic's governance practices**. While some view the Mythos containment as responsible behavior, others argue that failing to follow established RSP procedures undermines trust in industry self-regulation. This has sparked broader discussions about whether voluntary commitments are sufficient for AI governance.

There's also ongoing discussion about **research priorities and methodologies**. The model organism critique has generated debate about how to design experiments that better reflect real-world AI development conditions, with implications for how much confidence the community should place in current alignment research.

The **cybersecurity implications** of Mythos have prompted discussions about preparedness and the timeline for AI-enabled cyber threats. Some argue this vindicates concerns about rapid capability development, while others focus on the practical implications for securing critical infrastructure and online services.

Additionally, [**community outreach efforts**](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/2HfuvYzRZgHpLAgpe/help-me-launch-obsolete-a-book-aimed-at-building-a-new) continue with attempts to broaden AI safety concerns beyond technical communities, including new books aimed at building broader movements for AI reform.
