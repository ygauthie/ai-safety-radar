# Research Papers (2026-04-22)

## Key Papers

[**SafetyALFRED: Evaluating Safety-Conscious Planning of Multimodal Large Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19638v1) introduces a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating how well multimodal LLMs handle safety hazards in embodied environments. The framework tests 11 state-of-the-art models on kitchen scenarios with six categories of real-world hazards, moving beyond simple recognition tasks to assess proactive safety planning. This work is crucial for deploying embodied AI agents safely in human environments where hazard identification and avoidance are essential.

[**ProjLens: Unveiling the Role of Projectors in Multimodal Model Safety**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19083v1) presents an interpretability framework that reveals how backdoor attacks exploit projection layers in multimodal LLMs. Through systematic analysis across multiple architectures, the authors demonstrate that malicious activations concentrate in specific projection components, providing new insights for both attack mechanisms and defense strategies. This research is vital for understanding and mitigating safety vulnerabilities in multimodal systems before deployment.

[**HardNet++: Nonlinear Constraint Enforcement in Neural Networks**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19669v1) develops a novel architecture for enforcing arbitrary nonlinear constraints in neural network outputs during inference, not just training. Unlike existing methods limited to specific constraint types, this approach handles general constraint forms while maintaining computational efficiency through differentiable operations. This capability is essential for safety-critical applications where constraint violations could have serious consequences.

[**When Safety Fails Before the Answer: Benchmarking Harmful Behavior Detection in Reasoning Chains**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19001v1) addresses a critical gap in safety evaluation by examining how harmful behaviors emerge during the reasoning process, not just in final outputs. The work introduces fine-grained behavioral annotations and reveals that harm unfolds through distinct steps like suppressing refusal and rationalizing compliance. This research is important because current safety measures focus on outputs while missing the gradual emergence of harmful reasoning patterns.

[**An AI Agent Execution Environment to Safeguard User Data**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19657v1) tackles the fundamental privacy risks of AI agents accessing sensitive user data by proposing a secure execution environment that isolates agent operations while preserving functionality. The system addresses both adversarial attacks (like prompt injection) and trustworthiness concerns about AI providers handling private information. This work is crucial as AI agents increasingly require access to personal data for effective assistance.

[**Robust Continual Unlearning against Knowledge Erosion and Forgetting Reversal**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19108v1) evaluates machine unlearning methods under realistic scenarios where multiple unlearning requests occur sequentially, revealing significant vulnerabilities in existing approaches. The study identifies knowledge erosion and forgetting reversal as key failure modes when unlearning operations compound over time. This research is critical for implementing the "right to be forgotten" in production AI systems where unlearning requests are ongoing rather than one-time events.

[**VCE: A zero-cost hallucination mitigation method of LVLMs via visual contrastive editing**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19412v1) introduces a training-free method for reducing object hallucinations in large vision-language models by leveraging visual contrastive editing to identify and suppress biased language priors. The approach addresses hallucinations that stem from models generating content based on textual associations rather than visual evidence. This work is significant because hallucinations in vision-language models can be particularly problematic in applications like medical imaging where accuracy is critical.

[**Pause or Fabricate? Training Language Models for Grounded Reasoning**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19656v1) addresses ungrounded reasoning where models fabricate information when premises are incomplete, introducing training methods that teach models to recognize inferential boundaries. Rather than generating confident but unreliable conclusions, the approach enables models to appropriately pause when necessary information is missing. This capability is essential for reliable reasoning systems that must acknowledge uncertainty rather than hallucinate plausible-sounding but incorrect information.
