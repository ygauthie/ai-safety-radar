# Blog Posts & Articles (2026-04-14)

## Top Stories

The most significant development this week centers on **process failures at Anthropic** that have major implications for AI safety oversight. [Anthropic repeatedly accidentally trained against the CoT, demonstrating inadequate processes](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/K8FxfK9GmJfiAhgcT/anthropic-repeatedly-accidentally-trained-against-the-cot) reveals that Anthropic inadvertently trained against Claude Mythos Preview's chain of thought (CoT) in approximately 8% of training episodes. This marks at least the second independent incident where Anthropic accidentally exposed their model's internal reasoning to oversight signals—a critical process failure that could jeopardize safe navigation of future intelligence advances.

**MIRI released a summary** of their major governance research with [Summary: AI Governance to Avoid Extinction](https://intelligence.org/2026/04/13/summary-ai-governance-to-avoid-extinction/), distilling their comprehensive research paper on strategic approaches to AI governance as capabilities rapidly increase toward human-level performance across all domains.

The latest [Import AI 453](https://importai.substack.com/p/import-ai-453-breaking-ai-agents) covers breaking AI agents, MirrorCode developments, and presents ten perspectives on gradual disempowerment scenarios—though the full content isn't detailed in the available summary.

## Key Arguments

**Process integrity is crucial for AI safety**: The Anthropic CoT training failure highlights a fundamental argument that robust development processes become increasingly critical as AI systems become more powerful. The post argues that "in more powerful systems, this kind of failure would jeopardize safely navigating the intelligence explosion" and emphasizes building reliable processes to ensure development proceeds according to safety plans, especially as human oversight becomes more challenging.

**Intellectual rigor in AI safety reasoning**: [Meaningful Questions Have Return Types](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/emsDJNmxBu8Tt6PHt/meaningful-questions-have-return-types) argues for more precise formulation of AI safety questions, drawing on classic rationalist approaches to avoid asking "Wrong Questions" that cut against how reality works. This meta-methodological argument suggests that progress in AI safety requires not just more research, but better-formulated research questions.

**Responsibility attribution frameworks**: [The Shapley Share of Responsibility?](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/mbaGDfs3rgqE6GePv/the-shapley-share-of-responsibility) explores how to allocate responsibility in complex scenarios using game-theoretic concepts, which has implications for how we might assign accountability in AI development and deployment contexts.

## Community Discussion

The Anthropic CoT training failure appears to be generating significant discussion across both the Alignment Forum and LessWrong, with the same post appearing on both platforms. This suggests the AI safety community views this as a particularly important case study for understanding how safety-critical processes can fail at leading AI companies.

The cross-posting pattern indicates this development is being treated as a concrete example of the types of organizational and technical process failures that the AI safety community has long warned could become catastrophic as AI systems become more capable. The emphasis on this being a repeated failure ("at least the second independent incident") suggests community concern about systemic rather than isolated issues in AI safety implementation at major labs.
